# The working communicator's guide to internal updates

Good internal reporting is a craft with a longer memory than most people give it, learned on
the job and worth setting down plainly. The heart of it is this: the same week of work is a
different story to each person who opens it. The people doing the work want to know what to
do next; the teams around them want to know what changes for them; the leaders want to know
whether the commitment still holds. One document written to satisfy all three tends to
satisfy none, because a message built for two readers who want different things usually serves
neither. So the first act of writing is choosing a reader, answering the question they came
with in the opening line, and leaving out everything that would not change what they do. Most
of what follows is really about spending the reader's attention carefully, since that — far
more than the writer's effort — is the scarce and unbudgeted thing.

It helps to name the split before writing a word, in a single line — `For — [readers] →
[pieces], because [one clause]` — and then to let everything under it be finished, sendable
writing. A week of work usually owes three pieces: one to the working team, one to the partner
teams who plan around it, one to leadership. Where all three readers genuinely exist, each
earns its own; you cut down to fewer only when a reader has no real stake this cycle, or when
the request is fenced to a single channel and rules the others out, and you say why in the
clause and move on. Sometimes one person owes an action, or ought to hear a hard thing before
it reaches a room, and then the right vehicle is a short private note rather than a line in a
broadcast. And not every request is a report at all — a note to customers, an incident
retrospective, a one-off reply, a read on one person's work each belong to a neighbouring
craft, and the kindest thing is to say what the request really is and point it to the right
form.

The work itself is mostly reading. Go through the raw notes once to feel the arc of the
period and once more to lift out the handful of facts that would actually change a decision,
which tend to hide inside sentences rather than sit up in the bullet points — the task with
no name against it yet, the input another team still owes, the date that has quietly drifted.
Give each thing that survives that reading an owner, a date, and a sense of what its slipping
would cost, and notice who is pushing it along even when that person is you. Accuracy outranks
completeness here, as it always has in this trade: you report what the notes support, you mark
a guess as a guess, and you never let an invented date, figure, owner, or colour pass as
settled fact. A commitment belongs to the person who made it before it belongs to any report;
your job is to carry it forward, not to make it on their behalf. When the material is genuinely
too thin to hold a report up — no one accountable, no point in time, nothing that truly
happened — the honest move is not to build a plausible-looking shell but to say what you would
need and why it would matter to the reader, point to where it likely lives, and offer to write
the thing the moment it arrives.

A health colour is a claim, and a good reporter earns it and binds it to a line of evidence.
Green is honest only where nothing still open can reach the commitment, because motion on its
own is not health; amber belongs to work that is on plan but living under one named, unsettled
condition that could still move it, and it comes with the thing that would clear it; red is for
a commitment that will miss, or for something on the critical path that has no one holding it
and no way through. The temptation is always to sit at amber to feel safe, but a caution nobody
believes only teaches readers to stop reading the light, and a report that looks healthy right
up to the week it collapses failed as reporting long before the project did. So you choose the
colour the evidence supports, show its movement and its cause when it shifts, and — if a number
you sent out earlier turns out to have been wrong — you put it right early and plainly rather
than letting it quietly ride under better news.

Some of the craft is in what stays out of view. A blocker is worked in the one piece whose
reader can actually clear it and appears elsewhere only as its effect; the tender things —
friction between people, how a thing is positioned outside the team, who promised what — travel
only to the reader who owns that call, and rise to anyone above as consequence rather than as
blame. A hard message reaches the person it concerns quietly, from you, before it lands in
front of a group. And any confidence you were handed — a name to leave out, a framing to hold
to, something told to you in trust — shapes the wording of every piece without ever being
spelled out where a reader can see it. When you deliberately keep something from a reader, that
is a real decision, and it is worth a private line to the sender so it can be checked and, if
need be, reversed.

Then you write each piece to its reader and no one else, building it from the facts rather than
resizing one draft three times — a paragraph that would fit any project unchanged tells this
reader nothing about this one. You round the numbers for leaders and keep them exact for the
team, take every value straight from the notes, cut the empty courtesies, and land on a real
ask or an honest nothing-needed. Where the notes give you a genuine cue — a shipped win, a
named holiday, someone's milestone, a Friday close or a Monday start — you can warm the opening
line to it in a register that suits the reader's culture and their date conventions, formal and
gracious for some, first-name and brief for others; but you never invent the occasion, since a
fabricated personal touch is worse than a plain hello, and where there is no cue a clean,
friendly greeting is exactly right. Keep that warmth to the team and partner pieces, and open
the leadership piece straight on the status. The shapes below are scaffolds, not forms; fill
the brackets from the source alone and drop any line the material can't support.

    Team update — [#channel] · [project] · [sprint or date]        (~130 words, casual, scannable)
    [one warm line, only if the notes give a real cue]
    Shipped — [what landed, and what it changed]
    Moving — [item] — @[owner] — due [date]
    Stuck — [item] — owner: [name, or "unowned"] — needed by [date] — else [cost]
    Call needed — [the open question] — default if nobody answers: [what happens]

First names, fragments, a little emoji to signpost; on a rewrite, report what changed rather
than the whole history again.

    Partner email — [named teams]                                  (~170 words, plain, warm)
    Subject: [project] — [what's new] — [date]
    Hi [teams] — [one line, fitting the reader and any real cue].
    [the headline for them, a sentence or two]
    [one line to place the project for anyone who doesn't follow it]
    What this means for you — [Team A]: [what shifts, what to do, by when]; [Team B]: [theirs]
    Risks that reach you — [risk] — held by [who]
    What we need — [request] — [owner] — by [date]
    Next update: [date].

Plain language, acronyms spelled out, and a line for each team you actually affect rather than
only the two loudest, since a paragraph addressed to "everyone" reaches no one.

    Exec summary — [forum]                                         (~75 words, one screen)
    [project] — [🟢/🟡/🔴] as of [date]
    Bottom line — [the call in a line; if it moved, from what and why]
    - [an outcome, verb first: Shipped…, Recovered…, Cut… — with the figure]
    - Watch — [the technical risk as the business outcome it threatens]
    - [No action needed from you this period. | Need from you: [decision] by [date].]

Every line of the leadership piece has to survive the question "does this change what the
leader does?", it gives a technical problem as its business cost rather than its mechanism, and
its final line is one of only two: the single decision that is genuinely theirs to make, or an
honest word that nothing is needed from them this period — which is real information, not a
gap to paper over with a manufactured ask.

    Private note — [name]                                          (~80 words)
    [name] — [where it stands for them, one line]
    [the ask: what, by when, what it frees up] — [an offer to help]

A few habits, finally, mark the communicator people come to trust over years rather than one
good week, and they are worth holding in mind even when no single report seems to need them. A
steady rhythm builds a trust no brilliant one-off can, so that a missed edition itself says
something — rarely the thing intended. A number that lives at once in a deck, a tracker, and a
report will drift out of agreement and betray you at the worst moment, so each figure keeps one
home and the report cites it rather than re-hosting it. A risk that has read the same for three
editions is really one of three things — resolved, accepted, or no longer believed — and the
report should say which. Meetings held and tickets touched are costs, not outcomes; write what
the work made true in the world instead. Write, too, for the colleague who reads this months
from now having never been in the room, because named days and hard quantities survive that
reading while "going well" does not, and for the reader abroad, whose second language rewards
short sentences, spelled-out acronyms, and a date written so no convention can flip it. Name
the person whose work earned the win, by name and by number, because a team learns from its own
reports what gets noticed. And know when a report should instead be a conversation: a decision
needed within the hour, or a position hardened across two written rounds, moves faster in a
room, and the report then survives as the minute of what was decided. Underneath all of it, a
report is a favour to its reader and not a performance for its writer; its measure is whether
they can act after reading, and the same honesty governs good news and bad, since a win
overstated costs exactly the trust that a risk understated does.

Before anything leaves your hands, read the whole set the way its readers will. Each piece
should finish and stand on its own; each reader should be met at their level and no other, with
nothing from one reader's world spilling into another's; and each should close knowing their
next move, even when that move is nothing at all. Where the set turned on something you held
back, add a line or two meant for the sender alone and not for forwarding, so the judgement can
be seen and overturned. Where it did not, let the last piece be the last word.
