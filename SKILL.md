# The reader-first status update

One week of work reads as three different stories. The people doing it need to know what to
do next; the teams downstream need to know what shifts for them; the leaders need to know
whether the commitment still holds. Write to one of them at a time, put the answer they came
for in the first line, and leave out everything that doesn't change what they do.

## What the reply looks like

Give, in order: a one-line routing call, then the finished pieces, then — only where you
deliberately kept something from a reader — two closing lines meant for the sender. Finish
each piece in full before starting the next; a piece that stops half-written helps no one.
Hold to the word count under each template, and when you are short of room, tighten the
sentences rather than dropping a piece.

## Choose the readers first

Name the split before you write a word:

`For — [readers] → [pieces], because [one clause].`

A week of work usually owes three pieces — one to the working team, one to the partner teams
who plan around it, one to leadership — and where all three readers are real, each gets its
own; a single document meant for all of them serves none. Drop a piece only when its reader
has no stake this cycle, or when the request is fenced to one channel and shuts the others
out — name the reason in the clause. Add a short private note to one person when they owe an
action, or when hard news should reach them before it reaches a room.

Some requests aren't status reports at all — a note to customers, an incident write-up, a
quick reply, a read on one person's performance. Name what they actually are and stop. And
when the raw material is too thin to stand a report up honestly, don't prop up a hollow one:
say what you'd need and why it would matter to the reader, say where it likely lives, and
offer to write it the moment it arrives.

## Find the facts that carry weight

Go through the notes twice: first for the shape of the stretch — a launch, a slip, a number
corrected, a dependency forming, or ordinary progress — then for the handful of facts that
would change someone's decision, which tend to sit inside sentences rather than in the
bullet list: the task with no name against it, the input another team still owes, the date
that has quietly moved. Give each one an owner, a date, and what its slipping costs; for
anything stuck, add what happens when that date passes. Write only what the notes support,
flag a guess as a guess, and let no invented date, figure, owner, or colour stand as fact.
You carry other people's commitments forward; you don't make them on their behalf.

## Commit the colour, and defend it

Bind the traffic-light to one line of evidence.

- **🟢 Green** — nothing still open can reach the commitment. A fault that is contained,
  owned, and off the path to the deadline stays green; dropping to amber to look careful is
  the most common way to make a colour worthless.
- **🟡 Amber** — the work is on plan, but one named, unresolved thing could still move the
  commitment. Name it, and name what would clear it.
- **🔴 Red** — the commitment will miss, or something on the critical path has no owner and no
  way through. An unheld critical item is red now, not on the day it finally breaks.

Show the colour's move when it moves, and its cause. A number you gave out wrong is put right
in the open, at the top, ahead of any good news. And the reader who owns a piece of bad news
hears it in private before it lands in a group.

## Handle the delicate parts with care

Work each blocker in the one piece whose reader can clear it; elsewhere it appears only as
its effect. Keep anything sensitive — tension between people, who promised what, how
something is positioned outside the team — to the one reader who owns that call, and render
it upward as consequence, not blame ("a resourcing risk on the data workstream," not a
name). A missed handoff is a quiet word to the person first, and no more than a neutral,
dated line in anything broadcast. Any instruction you were handed — a name to keep out of the
exec tier, a framing to hold to, a confidence to protect — lives in the wording of every
piece, subject lines and openings included, and is pointed at nowhere a reader can see.

## Write each piece to its reader

Build each from the facts, not by resizing one draft three times — a paragraph that would fit
any project tells the reader nothing about this one. Round the numbers for leaders and keep
them exact for the team, and pull every value straight from the notes. Warm the first line to
the moment wherever the notes give you a real cue — a shipped win, a named holiday, someone's
milestone, a Friday close or a Monday start — in the register that suits the reader's culture
and their date conventions; never invent the occasion. On a rewrite, lead with what changed,
not the whole story again.

### Team update — working channel (~130 words, casual, scannable)

    [#channel] · [project] · [sprint or date]
    [one warm line, only if the notes give a real cue]
    Shipped — [what landed, and what it changed]
    Moving — [item] — @[owner] — due [date]
    Stuck — [item] — owner: [name, or "unowned"] — needed by [date] — else [cost]
    Call needed — [the open question] — default if nobody answers: [what happens]

First names, fragments, a little emoji to signpost.

### Partner email — the teams who depend on you (~170 words, plain, warm)

    Subject: [project] — [what's new] — [date]
    Hi [teams] — [one line, fitting the reader and any real cue].
    [the headline for them, a sentence or two]
    [one line to place the project for anyone who doesn't follow it]
    What this means for you — [Team A]: [what shifts, what to do, by when]; [Team B]: [theirs]
    Risks that reach you — [risk] — held by [who]
    What we need — [request] — [owner] — by [date]
    Next update: [date].

Plain language, acronyms spelled out, and a line for every team you actually touch — not just
the two loudest; a paragraph to "everyone" reaches no one.

### Exec summary — leadership (~75 words, one screen, outcome first)

    [project] — [🟢/🟡/🔴] as of [date]
    Bottom line — [the call in a line; if it moved, from what and why]
    - [an outcome, verb first: Shipped…, Recovered…, Cut… — with the figure]
    - Watch — [the technical risk as the business outcome it threatens]
    - [No action needed from you this period. | Need from you: [decision] by [date].]

The exec piece opens straight on the status line — no greeting. Every line has to survive
"does this change what the leader does?" The last line is one of the two shown: when
leadership genuinely needs to do nothing, say so — that is information, and inventing a
decision to seem urgent spends the attention you can least afford. Give a technical problem
as its business cost, not its mechanism.

### Private note — one person, where a broadcast would be wrong (~80 words)

    [name] — [where it stands for them, one line]
    [the ask: what, by when, what it frees up] — [an offer to help]

## The last look

Read the set the way its readers will. Each piece finishes and stands on its own. Each reader
is met at their level and no other, with nothing from one reader's world spilling into
another's, and each is left knowing their next move — including when that move is nothing.
Where you deliberately held something back, close with two lines for the sender alone, not to
be forwarded, so the call can be seen and reversed.
