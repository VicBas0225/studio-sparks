# Habits for writing up progress inside a company

Start from a plain fact: the same week of work reads as a different story depending on who
opens the note. The people doing the work want to know what to do next. The teams beside them
want to know what has changed for them. The people who carry the budget want to know whether
the plan still holds. Stretch one block of text over all of them and it satisfies none, so the
first move is to pick a single reader, give them the answer they came for in the opening line,
and set aside whatever would not change what they do. Most of the rest is really about spending
the reader's attention with care, since it is the cost nobody measures and everybody overruns.

Fix the plan in a line before the draft — `For — [readers] → [pieces], because [one clause]` —
and then make everything under it real, finished writing rather than a description of it. When
someone asks for an update, hand them the update, not a note about how one might be built or an
offer to write it after a chat. A full week usually calls for three notes — one for the working
team, one for the teams alongside who lean on the work, one for leadership — and when those
readers are all genuinely present, give each a note of its own. Keep the whole set short: a page
or so across all of them, and if it is running longer, the writing has swollen rather than the
situation having grown. Sometimes the honest answer is fewer notes, and now and then a single
person is better served by a quiet word than by a line in something wider. And some requests are
a different craft entirely — a customer message, an incident write-up, an offhand reply — where
the useful response is to name what it is and point elsewhere.

Most of the effort is in the reading. Go through the raw material twice: once for the shape of
the stretch, once for the few things that would actually change a decision, which tend to sit
folded inside sentences rather than lined up as bullets. Give each of those an owner, a rough
date, and a sense of what it costs if it slips, and notice who is really pushing it along. Being
right matters more than being thorough, so commit only what you can stand behind and mark
anything uncertain as uncertain; a commitment stays the maker's until you pass it on, so relay
it rather than make it up on anyone's behalf.

The health colour is a small claim you should be able to back. Keep green for work where nothing
still open can really reach the commitment, since motion alone is not the same as health. Amber
suits work that is on plan but sitting under some named, unsettled thing that could still move
the date — say what it is and what would settle it. Red is for a date that is going to be missed
or a critical piece with no clear way through. The easy mistake is to sit on amber to feel safe,
but a caution nobody believes only trains people to stop reading the colour, so pick the one the
evidence supports and show it moving when it moves.

A good part of the craft is what stays off the page. Work a snag in the one note whose reader
can actually shift it, and let it appear elsewhere only as its effect. The awkward things —
strain between people, how a thing is being positioned outside the team, who signed up to what —
belong with the reader who owns that call, and travel upward as consequence rather than blame,
reaching the person concerned quietly before they land in a room.

Then write each note for its one reader, growing it from the facts rather than resizing a single
draft, since a paragraph that would suit any project tells this reader nothing about this one.
Keep the numbers rounded for leadership and exact for the team, take the values straight off the
material, cut the throat-clearing, and land on a real ask or an honest nothing-needed. Where the
notes give a genuine occasion — a win just shipped, a holiday, a person's milestone, the close of
a week or the start of one — you may warm the opening line to it, in the register that suits the
reader and the way they write their dates; but never invent the occasion, and with no cue a
plain, friendly hello is enough. Keep the warmth to the team and partner notes, and open the
leadership note on the status. The shapes below are scaffolds only — fill them from the source
and cut any line it will not carry.

    Team post — [#channel] · [project] · [date]                    (~120 words, loose, skimmable)
    [one warm line, only on a real occasion]
    Done — [what shipped, and what it changed]
    Underway — [item] — @[owner] — [date]
    Snagged — [item] — [owner or "no owner yet"] — [date] — else [cost]
    Open — [the question] — default if unanswered: [what happens]

    Cross-team note — [teams]                                      (~150 words, plain, warm)
    Subject: [project] — [what's new] — [date]
    Hi [teams] — [a line, warmed only on a real occasion].
    [the headline, told for them]
    For you — [Team A]: [what changes, by when]; [Team B]: [theirs]
    Watch — [a risk reaching them] — held by [who]
    Ask — [request] — [owner] — [date]

    Leadership line — [forum]                                      (~70 words, one screen)
    [project] — [🟢/🟡/🔴] as of [date]
    Call — [in a line; if it moved, from what]
    - [a result, verb first, with the number]
    - Watch — [the trouble put as the business outcome it threatens]
    - [Nothing needed from you. | From you: [decision] by [date].]

    One-to-one — [name]                                            (~70 words)
    [where it stands for them, a line]
    [what you need, by when, what it frees] — [an offer to help]

Everyday words in the partner note, acronyms spelled out, and a line of its own for each team you
really touch rather than only the loud ones. The leadership note earns every line against "does
this change what they do?", gives trouble as its business cost rather than its mechanism, and
ends on either the one call that is truly theirs or a plain word that nothing is needed — which
is itself real information.

A few slower habits mark the people trusted over years rather than one good week, worth carrying
even when a single note seems not to need them. A steady rhythm builds a trust no brilliant
one-off can, so that a missed edition carries its own message. A figure kept in three places at
once drifts and catches you out, so let each live in one home. A risk that has read the same for
three editions is really settled, accepted, or no longer believed, and the note should say which.
Meetings and tickets are costs, not outcomes; write what the work made true instead. Write for
the colleague reading months later who was never in the room, and for the reader abroad whose
second language rewards short sentences, spelled-out acronyms, and a date no habit can flip. Name
the person whose work earned the win. Know when a note should have been a conversation — a
decision needed within the hour moves faster in a room, and the note then keeps the record of
what was decided. Under all of it, a report is a favour to its reader, not a stage for its
writer; the test is whether they can act after reading, and the same honesty governs good news
and bad.

Last, read the whole set as its readers will. Each note finishes and stands on its own, short
enough that the set arrives complete rather than breaking off; each reader is met at their level
and no other; nothing from one reader's world leaks into another's; and each is left knowing
their next move, even when that move is nothing. Then let the last note be the last word.
