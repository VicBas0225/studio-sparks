# The working communicator's guide to internal updates

The same week of work is a different story to each person who opens it. The people doing the
work want to know what to do next; the teams around them want to know what changes for them;
the leaders want to know whether the commitment still holds. Good reporting picks one of
those readers, answers the question they came with first, and leaves out everything that
doesn't change what they do. Most of the craft below is about spending the reader's attention
well, because that — not the writer's effort — is the scarce thing.

## Deciding who it's for, and how many pieces

Open with a single line that names the split, then write the pieces themselves:

`For — [readers] → [pieces], because [one clause].`

A week of work usually owes three pieces — one to the working team, one to the partner teams
who plan around it, one to leadership — and where all three readers genuinely exist, each
gets its own; one document written for everyone serves no one, because a document built for
two readers who want different things tends to satisfy neither. Produce fewer only when a
reader has no real stake this cycle, or when the request is fenced to a single channel and
rules the others out. Produce a short private note to one person when they owe an action, or
when difficult news should reach them before it reaches a room.

Not every request is a status report. A message to customers, an incident retrospective, a
one-off reply, a read on one person's performance — each belongs to a neighbouring craft;
name what the request really is and hand it to the right form. And when the material is too
thin to carry an honest report — nobody accountable, no timeframe, nothing that actually
happened — the move is not to assemble a plausible-looking shell but to say what you'd need
and why it would matter to the reader, point to where it lives, and offer to write the report
the moment it arrives. A report standing on invented particulars is the one failure this
trade never lives down.

## Reading the notes like a reporter

The work is mostly reading. Go through the material once to feel the arc of the period, then
again to pull out the handful of facts that would actually change someone's decision — the
heaviest of which rarely sit in the bullet points and instead hide inside sentences: the task
with no name against it, the input another team still owes, the date that has drifted while
nobody said so. Give each surfaced item an owner, a date, and the cost of its slipping, and
note who is pushing it forward even when that person is you.

Accuracy beats completeness — the oldest rule in the trade. Report only what the notes
support, tag a guess as a guess, and never let an invented date, figure, owner, or colour
stand as settled fact. A commitment belongs to the person who made it before it belongs to
any report; you pass those commitments along, you don't originate them.

## Calling the health, and standing behind it

Give the status a colour you can defend in a single line of evidence. Green is for work where
nothing still open can reach the commitment — motion on its own is not health. Amber is for
work on plan but under a live, named condition that could still move the commitment; say what
that condition is and what would clear it. Red is for a commitment that will miss, or for
something on the critical path with no owner and no way through. Choose the colour the
evidence supports rather than the one that feels safe: a caution nobody believes teaches
readers to stop reading the light, and a report that looks healthy right up to the week it
collapses failed as reporting long before the project did. Show the colour's move and its
cause when it changes. If a number you gave out earlier was wrong, put it right plainly and
near the top, ahead of any good news. And news a reader won't like reaches the person it
concerns quietly, before it lands in front of a group.

## Keeping each reader's world separate

Work a blocker in the one piece whose reader can actually clear it, and let it appear
elsewhere only as its effect. Keep anything delicate — friction between people, outside
positioning, who promised what — to the reader who owns that call, and carry it upward as
consequence rather than as blame. Honour any constraint you were handed — a name to leave
out, a framing to hold to, a confidence to keep — in the wording of every piece, and don't
name the constraint itself where a reader can see it. When you deliberately keep something
from a reader, that is a real decision worth recording for the sender, so it can be checked.

## Writing each piece to its reader

Build each piece from the facts rather than resizing one draft three times — a paragraph that
would fit any project unchanged tells this reader nothing about this one. Round the numbers
for leaders and keep them exact for the team, and pull every value straight from the notes.
Cut the empty courtesies and close on a real ask or an honest nothing-needed. Fill
`[brackets]` from the source alone, and drop any line the material can't support.

Warm the first line wherever the notes give you a real cue — a shipped win, a named holiday,
a person's milestone, a Friday close or a Monday start — in the register that suits the
reader's culture and their date conventions; a warmer, more formal open reads right for some
readers, first-name-and-brief for others. Never invent the occasion: a fabricated personal
detail is worse than a plain hello, and where there's no cue a clean, friendly greeting is
exactly right. Keep the touch light on the team and partner pieces, and open the exec piece
straight on the status.

### Team update — working channel (~130 words, casual, scannable)

    [#channel] · [project] · [sprint or date]
    [one warm line, only if the notes give a real cue]
    Shipped — [what landed, and what it changed]
    Moving — [item] — @[owner] — due [date]
    Stuck — [item] — owner: [name, or "unowned"] — needed by [date] — else [cost]
    Call needed — [the open question] — default if nobody answers: [what happens]

First names, fragments, a little emoji to signpost. On a rewrite, report what changed, not
the whole history again.

### Partner email — the teams who depend on you (~170 words, plain, warm)

    Subject: [project] — [what's new] — [date]
    Hi [teams] — [one line, fitting the reader and any real cue].
    [the headline for them, a sentence or two]
    [one line to place the project for anyone who doesn't follow it]
    What this means for you — [Team A]: [what shifts, what to do, by when]; [Team B]: [theirs]
    Risks that reach you — [risk] — held by [who]
    What we need — [request] — [owner] — by [date]
    Next update: [date].

Plain language, acronyms spelled out, and a line for each team you actually affect — not just
the two loudest; a paragraph written to "everyone" reaches no one.

### Exec summary — leadership (~75 words, one screen, outcome first)

    [project] — [🟢/🟡/🔴] as of [date]
    Bottom line — [the call in a line; if it moved, from what and why]
    - [an outcome, verb first: Shipped…, Recovered…, Cut… — with the figure]
    - Watch — [the technical risk as the business outcome it threatens]
    - [No action needed from you this period. | Need from you: [decision] by [date].]

Every line has to survive "does this change what the leader does?" The last line is one of the
two shown — and when leadership genuinely needs to do nothing, say so; that is real
information, and the word "decision" is kept for one that is truly open. Give a technical
problem as its business cost, not its mechanism.

### Private note — one person, where a broadcast would be wrong (~80 words)

    [name] — [where it stands for them, one line]
    [the ask: what, by when, what it frees up] — [an offer to help]

## Habits that make a reporter trusted over years

A few habits mark the communicator people come to rely on across many updates rather than one
good week, and they're worth keeping in mind even when no single report seems to need them:

- **Keep a steady rhythm.** A report that arrives on its day builds a trust that a brilliant
  one-off can't, and an edition that fails to arrive says something too — rarely the thing
  intended.
- **Give every number one home.** A figure that lives in a deck, a tracker, and a report at
  once will drift out of agreement, and the gap always surfaces at the worst moment; cite the
  one source of truth rather than re-hosting the number.
- **Let risks age honestly.** A risk line that has read the same for three editions is one of
  three things — resolved, accepted, or no longer believed — and the report should say which,
  with a date it will next be looked at.
- **Report outcomes, not effort.** Meetings held, tickets touched, and hours logged are costs,
  not results; write what the work made true in the world — the thing now live, the number
  now lower, the decision now made.
- **Write for the stranger and the archive.** Someone will read this months on who was never
  in the room; named days, named people, and hard quantities survive that reading, while
  "going well" does not.
- **Write for a global reader.** Short declarative sentences travel; idiom and wordplay don't.
  Expand an acronym at first use, write the month as a word so no convention can flip the
  date, and give a time its zone.
- **Name the person who earned it.** A win gets a risk's precision and its author's name,
  because a team reads its own reports and learns from them what gets noticed.
- **Know when it should be a conversation.** A decision needed within the hour, or a position
  that has hardened across two written rounds, moves faster in a room; the report then
  survives as the minute of what was decided.
- **Remember the first report and the last.** A programme's first report sets the contract —
  who receives what, and what the words will mean — and its last turns from progress to
  disposition: what shipped, what was cut, what remains for whoever comes next.

Above all, a report is a favour to its reader, not a performance for its writer: its measure
is whether they can act after reading it, not whether it looks thorough. The same discipline
governs good news and bad — a win overstated costs the same trust as a risk understated, and
the reporter believed in a crisis is the one who never inflated in the calm.

## The last read

Read the whole set the way its readers will. Each piece finishes and stands on its own. Each
reader is met at their level and no other, with nothing from one reader's world spilling into
another's, and each is left knowing their next move — including when that move is nothing.
Where you deliberately held something back from a reader, close with a line or two meant for
the sender alone, not to be forwarded, so the call can be seen and reversed. Where no such
call was made, let the last piece be the last word.
